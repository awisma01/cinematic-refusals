About the Project

A digital humanities research project mapping cinematic resistance in Palestinian women's filmmaking.

Project Summary

Cinematic Counter-Cartographies

This project maps how Palestinian women filmmakers — principally Mai Masri, Annemarie Jacir, and Farah Nabulsi — create counter-cartographies through their cinematic treatment of Israeli checkpoints and surveillance infrastructure. Drawing on films released between 2000 and 2025, it argues that film and literature function as complementary counter-cartographic methods: while film captures the materiality of carceral space through architecture, movement, framing, and sound design, literature maps what surveillance cannot document — the interior consciousness, embodied memory, and emotional labor that sustain Palestinian life under occupation.

Central to the project is the concept of sumud — Palestinian steadfastness and creative persistence — as both a lived philosophy and an analytical framework. The digital archive tracks how sumud operates not as passive endurance but as active spatial, temporal, and cinematic refusal.

Methodologically, this is a digital humanities project that combines close formal analysis of film and literary texts with counter-cartographic spatial theory. Each checkpoint functions as a case study: a site that can be mapped geographically, traced across the film archive, and analyzed for how different directors film the same space differently over time. The digital format allows those three registers — geography, archive, and analysis — to exist in conversation rather than in sequence.

Abstract

This project maps how Palestinian women filmmakers — principally Mai Masri, Annemarie Jacir, and Farah Nabulsi — transform Israeli checkpoints and surveillance infrastructures into sites of cinematic resistance. Drawing on films released between 2000 and 2025, it argues that Palestinian cinema functions as counter-cartographic practice: refusing the spatial logic of occupation through formal strategies of framing, duration, ellipsis, and sound design. Central to the analysis is the concept of sumud — Palestinian steadfastness and creative persistence — as both a lived philosophy and an analytical framework. The digital archive maps five typologies of surveillance space across the cinematic record, demonstrating how feminist witnessing of carceral space prompts the generative imagination of Palestinian futures.

Theoretical Framework

Scholarly Foundations

Counter-Cartography and the Production of Space

Henri Lefebvre's challenge to move beyond static definitions of space — to see it as a contested product of social relations and power — is foundational here. Palestinian artists are not only contesting space but creating new imaginaries and alternative forms of spatial knowledge that surveillance infrastructures cannot capture. De Certeau's complementary formulation that "what the map cuts up, the story cuts across" positions Palestinian film and literature as acts of spatial refusal: by refusing the spatial logics imposed by occupation, these artists narrate space differently, and the act of re-narration becomes a form of political resistance. Eyal Weizman's concept of the "politics of verticality" in Hollow Land extends this framework materially, theorizing how Israeli control operates three-dimensionally through checkpoint infrastructure, settlement patterns, and aerial surveillance.

Surveillance, Race, and the Gendered Gaze

Simone Browne's Dark Matters provides the critical lens for understanding how surveillance is always racialized and always gendered — operating differently on different bodies and creating blind spots precisely through those differences. Palestinians do not experience surveillance uniformly: their racialization under occupation produces different levels of scrutiny and forms of vulnerability, and these asymmetries are precisely what Palestinian women filmmakers make visible. Julie Peteet's Space and Mobility in Palestine centers the everyday Palestinian body, showing how the permit system, road closures, and checkpoints are designed to control Palestinian mobility — and through that control, Palestinian life. Peteet's concept of "carceral time" — time weaponized through delay — is particularly important for understanding how Palestinian cinema uses duration as a formal argument.

Sumud: Steadfastness as Analytical Framework

Lena Meari's theorization of sumud is central to this project. For Meari, sumud is not passive endurance but active political confrontation — a "political being/becoming and continuous engagement with the flows and constraints of the colonial situation." Jane Busse advances this through her concept of "sumud as a spatial quotidian practice," showing how Palestinians generate alternative spatial knowledge simply by living and creating within occupied space. Saleem Haddad's three-part understanding of sumud — as record-keeping and revitalization, as bearing witness and collective memory, and as a radical and daring act of imagination — organizes the project's analytical framework. Palestinian women filmmakers do all three things simultaneously: they preserve, they witness, and they imagine.

Feminist Witnessing

Maria Lugones' concept of faithful witnessing frames this project's feminist methodology. To witness faithfully is an active political commitment: standing with oppressed people against powerful institutions, recognizing resistance even when it goes against the grain of dominant common sense, and refusing the narrow worldview that siding with oppression demands. Yomaira Figueroa-Vásquez extends Lugones' thinking by showing how cultural production not only represents faithful witnessing but performs it — literature and film becoming sites where artists build counter-archival records that dominant systems are not structured to recognize. Nadera Shalhoub-Kevorkian's concept of "infiltrated intimacies" shifts the focus from visible militarized spaces into the deeply private zones of Palestinian women's lives, showing how occupation shapes daily routines, relationships, safety, and reproductive decisions. Because women inhabit these intimate spaces, they can witness from the inside — capturing forms of knowledge that neither counter-cartography nor the checkpoint camera can hold.

Guiding Questions

What This Research Asks

  • How do camera angles refuse the surveillance gaze?
  • What do ellipses and offscreen space keep from view — and why does that matter politically?
  • How does editing create a counternarrative to security footage?
  • What is the politics of what these filmmakers choose not to show?
  • How does feminist witnessing of carceral space prompt the imagination of Palestinian futures?

Key Terms

Glossary of Concepts

Theoretical concepts, spatial frameworks, and critical vocabulary used across this archive. Hover over underlined terms in the analysis pages to see definitions in place. For the full interactive glossary, visit the Glossary page.

Sumud

Theoretical Meari; Busse; Haddad

Arabic: steadfastness, persistence, rootedness. Sumud is not passive endurance but active political confrontation — a "political being/becoming and continuous engagement with the flows and constraints of the colonial situation." Saleem Haddad identifies three registers: record-keeping, bearing witness, and radical imagination. Palestinian women filmmakers do all three simultaneously.

Counter-Cartography

Spatial de Certeau; Lefebvre

Representing space in ways that refuse official cartographic power. De Certeau: "what the map cuts up, the story cuts across." Palestinian film narrates the land from within rather than surveying it from above.

Carceral Continuum

Political Gilmore; Shalhoub-Kevorkian

Carceral logic does not confine itself to prisons or checkpoints but infiltrates domestic, intimate, and bodily space. The carceral is not a discrete institution but a continuous atmosphere that reorganizes all of life.

Ellipsis

Cinematic Film studies; project framework

A cut that omits — deliberately skipping over a moment. In Palestinian cinema, ellipsis protects subjects from becoming spectacle while still acknowledging that something has happened.

Faithful Witnessing

Feminist Lugones, Pilgrimages/Peregrinajes

An active political commitment to recognizing resistance even when it goes against dominant common sense. For Palestinian women artists, faithful witnessing becomes deeply spatial — attending to everything surveillance tries to erase.

Politics of Verticality

Spatial Weizman, Hollow Land (2012)

Weizman: Israeli control operates three-dimensionally through airspace, tunnels, and elevation differentials. The checkpoint embodies verticality in miniature — watchtowers and overhead cameras organize space as a hierarchy of visibility.
