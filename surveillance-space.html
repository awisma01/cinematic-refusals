Research Framework

The Space of Surveillance

Beyond the checkpoint: mapping the architectures, databases, durations, and intimacies through which Palestinian life is rendered legible, trackable, and controllable.

I. Architectural II. Digital III. Temporal IV. Intimate / Domestic V. Cinematic

Checkpoints are the most visible expression of colonial control, but they are only one node in a much wider infrastructure of surveillance. This section maps five typologies of surveillance space that appear across Palestinian cinema from 2000 to 2025.

Each typology names a distinct register in which Palestinian life is rendered legible to power: the stone and steel of architectural enclosure; the algorithmic logic of digital biometrics; the weaponized stillness of temporal waiting; the occupation of intimate and domestic space; and the camera's own complicity, or refusal, as a cinematic surveillance apparatus.

Palestinian women filmmakers do not simply represent these spaces. They devise formal strategies, angles, ellipses, duration, offscreen sound, that refuse to reproduce the surveillance gaze even as they document it.

Typology I

Architectural Space

Cells, cameras, corridors, cages, waiting rooms: the built environment as instrument of control.

The architecture of Israeli military control is not incidental to surveillance, it is surveillance in material form. Checkpoints are engineered spaces: turnstiles that click, caged corridors that funnel bodies into single file, watchtowers whose sight lines are calculated, waiting rooms whose sightlessness is equally deliberate.

Scholar Eyal Weizman has described this as "the politics of verticality" — a three-dimensional sovereignty expressed through walls, barriers, and checkpoints that carve space into zones of visibility and invisibility, passage and prohibition.

Palestinian women filmmakers engage this architecture with precise formal awareness. Where surveillance design produces legibility — the readable body, the classified permit — cinema can produce opacity: holding on a wall, refusing the establishing shot, framing a corridor from within rather than surveying it from above.

  • LegibilityThe architectural demand that bodies be classifiable, readable, and sorted by a security apparatus.
  • OpacityÉdouard Glissant's counter-concept: the right to refuse being made transparent to power.

Typology II

Digital Space

Databases, permits, biometric IDs, population registries — surveillance before and beyond physical encounter.

The most powerful Israeli surveillance of Palestinian life is often invisible on screen: it operates through databases, permit systems, biometric capture, and population registries that determine movement long before a body reaches a turnstile.

Scholars including Helga Tawil-Souri and Virginia Tilley have analyzed this infrastructure: the color-coded ID system, the Oslo-era permit regime, the digital population registry that determines who may claim residency in Jerusalem. These systems operate on a principle of anticipatory surveillanceanticipatory surveillanceControl enacted before physical encounter — through databases, permits, and classification systems that determine movement before a body reaches a checkpoint.Full definition →: the permit denied before the checkpoint is reached.

Cinema struggles with the digital: data is invisible, non-spectacular. Palestinian filmmakers address this through formal indirection — the unreturned call, the document read aloud, the waiting room whose cause is offscreen. The database appears as absence.

  • BiopoliticsFoucault / Mbembe: the governance of populations through life-making and life-withholding power.
  • DataficationConverting Palestinian bodies and movements into data points legible to the security apparatus.
  • NecropoliticsAchille Mbembe: sovereignty expressed as the decision over who may live and who is left to die.

Typology III

Temporal Space

Waiting, delay, uncertainty, anticipatory punishmentanticipatory punishmentThe penalty extracted before any formal act of punishment — living under constant threat, delay, and uncertainty at checkpoints and permit offices.Full definition → — time as a weapon and a site of resistance.

The checkpoint is not only a spatial boundary — it is a temporal weapon. Time at the checkpoint is confiscated: hours of waiting, the unpredictable click of the turnstile, the arbitrary extension of delay that teaches people their own subjection to a time they do not control. Tawil-Souri compares this to solitary confinement — not through walls, but through the suspension of time.

This temporal logic extends far beyond the checkpoint itself. The waiting room — at the permit office, the hospital, the consulate — reproduces the checkpoint's suspension without its steel. Uncertainty about permit renewal constitutes what scholars call anticipatory punishment: the penalty extracted before any action is taken.

Cinema is uniquely positioned to render duration. Palestinian filmmakers use long takes, static cameras, and deliberate slowness not as aesthetic indulgence but as formal argument: this is how long they wait. Time on screen becomes testimony.

Key Concepts

  • Anticipatory PunishmentThe penalty of living under threat, delay, and uncertainty before any formal act of punishment.
  • Temporal SovereigntyThe power to control not just space but when others may move through it.
  • Dead TimeTime imposed as punishment — waiting without purpose, duration as humiliation.
  • Long TakeCinematic device: uninterrupted duration that forces the viewer to experience waiting alongside the subject.
"People are trained to listen for the 'click' of the turnstiles and to accept that their fate and time is not under their control."
Helga Tawil-Souri

Typology IV

Intimate & Domestic Space

Homes, hospitals, family life — the occupation of private space, and cinema's claim on the right to intimacy.

Occupation does not stop at the checkpoint. It enters the home through night raids, through the surveillance of family members, through the impossibility of family reunification under permit regimes. It enters the hospital through permit denials that prevent patients from reaching care.

Palestinian women filmmakers are particularly attentive to this intimate register of surveillance. Where the checkpoint is legible as political space, the home, hospital, and kitchen are not — yet they are equally under occupation. Filming domesticity becomes an act of counter-mapping.

This attention constitutes what might be called a politics of the ordinary: the insistence that Palestinian daily life — love, family, food, labor — is both surveyable by power and irreducible to its surveillance logic.

Key Concepts

  • Intimate OccupationThe extension of colonial control into domestic, familial, and bodily space.
  • Politics of the OrdinaryThe assertion that daily life — love, food, labor — is both politically constrained and irreducible to those constraints.
  • Family ReunificationThe permit process (often denied) that governs whether Palestinian families may live together.
  • Right to FuturityThe claim — asserted by cinema — that Palestinian life has a future not defined by occupation.

3000 Nights (2015)

dir. Mai Masri

The prison becomes paradoxically domestic as Layal raises her baby inside. Maternity as refusal of carceral logic.

Farha (2021)

dir. Darin J. Sallam

The home as the last space of safety — and its violent entry. Filmed from within, the domestic becomes the unit of dispossession.

Children of Shatila (1998)

dir. Mai Masri

The refugee camp as permanent domestic space. Masri's camera stays at the level of children's faces — refusing to make the camp a spectacle.

"The checkpoint is a space where time is suspended, and the camera captures this suspension — but what the camera must also find is what endures inside that suspension."
Mai Masri

Typology V

Cinematic Space

How the camera reproduces surveillance logic — and the formal strategies through which it refuses to.

The camera is itself a surveillance technology. It records, classifies, archives. News footage of checkpoints often adopts the perspective of the watchtower: overhead, scanning, objectifying. The documentary impulse — to show, to witness, to expose — risks reproducing the very gaze it intends to critique.

Palestinian women filmmakers develop what we might call counter-surveillance aestheticscounter-surveillance aestheticsFormal cinematic strategies that refuse to deploy the camera as an instrument of control — averted angles, ellipses, offscreen sound, deliberate withholding.Full definition →: formal strategies that acknowledge the camera's power while refusing to deploy it as an instrument of control. These include the averted angle — filming beside rather than from above; the ellipsis — cutting before the moment of violation; offscreen sound — the checkpoint heard but not shown.

Suleiman's formulation is precise: occupation enters the film not as subject but as condition of the image. The counter-surveillance filmmaker does not photograph occupation — she photographs life under it, which is different.

  • Condition of the ImageSuleiman: occupation is not the subject of the frame but the condition that makes the frame possible.
  • Formal RefusalThe choice of angle, cut, or absence that constitutes political argument without showing the political object.
  • Right to OpacityGlissant: the ethical claim that not all lives need to be fully visible to be worthy of respect.

Synthesis

Feminist Witnessing and Palestinian Futures

The carceral continuumcarceral continuumThe understanding that carceral logic infiltrates domestic, intimate, and bodily space — occupation does not stop at the checkpoint.Full definition → does not confine itself to checkpoints and prisons — it infiltrates domestic, intimate, and bodily space. Palestinian women filmmakers witness from inside those spaces.

The concept of the carceral continuum connects all the works in this archive. Hagar Kotef and Merav Amir's analysis shows that checkpoints are never neutral or uniform spaces but operate through gendered logics that position Palestinian women's bodies as especially vulnerable to state control. Strip searches, forced waiting, interrogation about family members, the demand to remove clothing — these are not incidental indignities but part of how occupation disciplines and humiliates Palestinian women by making their bodies the site through which power is enacted.

Shalhoub-Kevorkian's work on "infiltrated intimaciesinfiltrated intimaciesShalhoub-Kevorkian: the occupation of domestic and bodily space — night raids, surveillance of family members, governance of birth and reproductive decisions.Full definition →" extends this analysis into the home. Occupation enters through night raids, through the surveillance of family members, through the impossibility of family reunification under permit regimes. Women live with a constant anticipation of intrusion — the late-night knock, the raid that disrupts the household, the interrogation that redraws the boundaries of privacy. Because Palestinian women artists inhabit these intimate spaces, they can witness from the inside: the fear after a raid, the emotional negotiations families make, the altered routines children develop, the sensory memories that linger after soldiers leave. These forms of knowledge never appear in military archives. They are structurally invisible.

The same cultural and religious norms that limit how Palestinian women appear in public create the exact blind spots that Israeli surveillance cannot reach. Browne's insight that surveillance is always racialized and gendered is crucial here: those blind spots become some of the most powerful counter-archival material in the archive. Palestinian women artists turn what surveillance is structurally unable to record into knowledge — feminist witnessingfeminist witnessingAn epistemological practice for seeing and preserving what the surveillance state cannot capture — the intimate, bodily, and relational dimensions of occupation.Full definition → as epistemological practice and method for seeing, documenting, and preserving what the surveillance state is literally not designed to capture.

Masri witnesses through the body; Shibli witnesses through interior consciousness; Bisan Owda witnesses in real time through social media documentation, claiming the title of Hakawatiya — traditional Arabic oral storyteller — within the context of genocide to position her witnessing within a long tradition of Palestinian cultural memory-making. What they all share is a commitment to working in the spaces surveillance does not see, and turning those moments into records that matter politically.

This is how feminist witnessing opens space for imagining Palestinian futures — not by laying out a vision of liberation but by insisting again and again that Palestinian life exceeds what occupation tries to reduce it to. Haddad's formulation of sumud as a radical and daring act of imagination, aligned with Vizenor's concept of survivancesurvivanceVizenor: active presence over absence — the continuities of stories as generative world-making rather than mere survival.Full definition → — active presence over absence, the continuities of stories — frames this generative dimension of the archive. The act of documentation is itself the imagination: not imagining a future abstractly, but insisting daily that there is a future in which this will be remembered and in which these filmmakers will have been witnesses, not just victims.

Key Concepts

  • Carceral Continuum The understanding that carceral logic does not end at the checkpoint or prison gate but infiltrates domestic, intimate, and bodily life.
  • Faithful Witnessing Lugones: an active political commitment to recognizing resistance even against dominant common sense, and refusing to align with oppression.
  • Survivance Vizenor: active presence over absence, the continuities of stories — aligned here with sumud as generative world-making rather than mere survival.
  • Necessary Imagination The capacity — generated through feminist witnessing — to imagine Palestinian futures that exceed what occupation tries to reduce Palestinian life to.

The Argument

The politics of omission runs through every film in this archive. Jacir uses ellipsis precisely: the bank robbery in Salt of This Sea cuts away before it completes. Masri blurs the doctor's face in 3000 Nights. Nabulsi keeps the camera on Yusef's face rather than the soldier's gun in The Present. Shibli's narrator in Minor Detail obsessively recovers an archive that chose not to record.

These formal choices are not simply aesthetic. They constitute an epistemological claim: that not everything needs to be made visible to matter, that the opacityopacityGlissant: the ethical claim that not all lives need to be transparent to power. The right to refuse being made fully readable and controllable.Full definition → is a political right, and that the demand for total visibility is itself a form of colonial violence. Édouard Glissant's concept of opacity — the ethical claim that not all lives need to be fully transparent to be worthy of respect — runs through this archive as both its subject and its formal principle.

  • The archive focuses on Palestinian women filmmakers. Male directors whose work engages checkpoints appear only in supporting references — a methodological boundary, not a claim about significance.
