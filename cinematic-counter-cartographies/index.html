About the Project

Context, methodology, and theoretical frameworks

Project Overview

Research Focus

This digital humanities project investigates how Palestinian women filmmakers create counter-cartographies through their cinematic treatment of Israeli checkpoints. Rather than simply documenting surveillance infrastructure, directors such as Mai Masri, Annemarie Jacir, and Farah Nablusi actively refuse, reframe, and subvert the visual logic of state surveillance.

By analyzing three to five significant checkpoints as they appear across multiple films released between 2000 and 2025, this project reveals how the same physical spaces are cinematically reimagined over time. Each checkpoint becomes a case study in cinematic resistance, demonstrating how formal choices—camera angles, ellipses, offscreen space, and editing patterns—constitute acts of refusal.

Context: Checkpoints and Cinema

Israeli military checkpoints punctuate Palestinian life, functioning as both physical barriers and instruments of surveillance. These sites are designed to monitor, control, and document Palestinian movement. In response, Palestinian cinema has developed sophisticated strategies for representing—and refusing—checkpoint spaces.

This project builds on scholarship in Palestinian cinema studies, surveillance studies, critical geography, and Third Cinema theory to examine how cinematic form itself becomes a tool of resistance.

Methodology

Research Approach

This project employs a mixed-methods approach combining close textual analysis, spatial mapping, and digital archival practices.

01

Film Selection & Analysis

Selection of Palestinian films (2000-2025) featuring significant checkpoint scenes, with particular attention to works by women directors.

02

Spatial Documentation

Mapping of checkpoint locations using GIS data combined with film analysis to create dual-layered maps.

03

Comparative Visual Analysis

Side-by-side comparison of how different directors frame identical checkpoint spaces.

04

Counter-Archival Practice

Documentation of what is deliberately not shown, exploring the politics of absence and the ethical dimensions of refusing complete visibility.
