Comparative Analysis

Camera Angles and Framing

Salt of This Sea (Jacir, 2008)

Jacir employs medium-long shots that position the camera at the eye level of Palestinian subjects waiting at Qalandiya. The framing refuses the elevated, panoptic perspective of security cameras. Instead, we see the checkpoint from within the queue—experiencing the spatial compression, the claustrophobic turnstiles, the sense of being funneled through narrow cages.

The camera remains largely static during checkpoint scenes, using long takes that emphasize duration and waiting. This formal choice resists the fragmentation typical of surveillance footage, instead presenting checkpoint passage as an extended temporal ordeal.

The Present (Nablusi, 2020)

Nablusi shoots Qalandiya in tighter close-ups and medium shots, bringing us even closer to the protagonist's experience. The camera is often handheld, creating a sense of instability and anxiety that mirrors the checkpoint's arbitrary violence.

Where Jacir uses static long takes, Nablusi employs more frequent cuts, but these cuts refuse surveillance logic—they disorient rather than clarify, emphasizing the Kafka-esque absurdity of checkpoint bureaucracy rather than imposing visual order.

Editing and Temporal Strategy

Salt of This Sea (Jacir, 2008)

Jacir's editing preserves real-time duration in checkpoint scenes. Minutes pass onscreen as characters wait, producing a temporal experience closer to documentary than fiction. The refusal to condense or ellide checkpoint time becomes an ethical choice: to show waiting is to refuse the erasure of Palestinian temporal experience.

The Present (Nablusi, 2020)

Nablusi similarly resists temporal compression but uses more cuts within checkpoint sequences. These cuts don't accelerate time but fragment it—emphasizing the repetitive, circular nature of checkpoint bureaucracy where progress is illusory and Palestinians are sent from one station to another without resolution.

Use of Offscreen Space

Both directors strategically employ offscreen space to refuse complete visibility. In Jacir's film, Israeli soldiers are often heard but not seen, or appear as silhouettes behind reflective glass. This formal choice mirrors the checkpoint's architecture of one-way vision: soldiers see Palestinians, but Palestinians cannot fully see soldiers.

Nablusi takes this further, frequently keeping soldiers entirely offscreen, reducing them to disembodied voices from loudspeakers. This strategy refuses to grant soldiers visual presence, instead emphasizing the faceless, bureaucratic nature of checkpoint control.

Sound Design

Sound plays a crucial role in both films' checkpoint scenes. The metallic clang of turnstiles, the harsh buzz of denial, the crackling commands from loudspeakers—these sonic elements create an oppressive auditory landscape.

Both directors occasionally deploy strategic silence, creating moments where ambient checkpoint noise drops out entirely. These silences are not peaceful but uncanny—brief interruptions in the relentless sonic assault of surveillance infrastructure.

Conclusion: Different Strategies, Shared Refusal

While Jacir and Nablusi employ distinct formal strategies—Jacir's static long takes versus Nablusi's handheld fragmentation—both refuse to replicate surveillance camera logic. They reject the elevated, omniscient checkpoint gaze in favor of subjective, ground-level perspectives that center Palestinian experience.

Their formal choices constitute what we might call "counter-surveillance cinematography": camera work that refuses the visual epistemology of state control, instead creating alternative visual logics grounded in Palestinian agency, dignity, and resistance.
