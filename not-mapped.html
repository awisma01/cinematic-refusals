What's Not Mapped

The politics of omission, the gaps in the archive, and the formal strategies of withholding that run through Palestinian women's cinema.

"Occupation is total: it enters the film not as a subject but as a condition of the image."
— Elia Suleiman
The Argument

The Politics of Omission

The title of this section names what may be the archive's most important methodological commitment. The politics of omission runs through every film analyzed here. Jacir uses ellipsis precisely: the bank robbery in Salt of This Sea cuts away before it completes. Masri blurs the doctor's face in 3000 Nights. Nabulsi keeps the camera on Yusef's face rather than the soldier's gun in The Present. Shibli's narrator in Minor Detail obsessively recovers an archive that chose not to record.

These formal choices are not simply aesthetic. They constitute an epistemological claim: that not everything needs to be made visible to matter, that the right to opacity is a political right, and that the demand for total visibility is itself a form of colonial violence. Édouard Glissant's concept of opacity — the ethical claim that not all lives need to be fully transparent to be worthy of respect — runs through the archive as both its subject and its formal principle.

The counter-surveillance filmmaker does not photograph occupation. She photographs life under it, which is different. This archive is an attempt to trace that difference — across checkpoints, across decades, across the work of filmmakers who have insisted, film by film, that Palestinian life exceeds the frame occupation tries to put around it.

Formal Strategies

How Filmmakers Withhold

Each film in the archive develops its own approach to what it refuses to show. The strategies below are not evasions — they are arguments made through formal choice, using the language of cinema to refuse the logic of surveillance.

Salt of This Sea

Jacir, 2008

Ellipsis

The bank robbery cuts away before completion. Jacir withholds the moment of violation to protect her subjects from becoming spectacle.

3000 Nights

Masri, 2015

Anonymization

The doctor's face is blurred, reducing the medical encounter to anonymous hands. Medicine becomes an extension of the carceral system rather than a human act.

The Present

Nabulsi, 2020

Averted angle

The camera stays on Yusef's face, not the soldier's gun. The violence is formal: heard and felt but refused the image it would want.

When I Saw You

Jacir, 2012

Unresolved ending

We do not see whether Tarek crosses. The camera stays with uncertainty, refusing the narrative closure that would turn longing into a contained story.

Minor Detail

Shibli, 2017

Counter-archival labor

The narrator recovers what the official archive treated as a minor detail. The novel's act of witnessing is inseparable from what the original record refused to preserve.

Divine Intervention

Suleiman, 2002

Static refusal

Static takes, no dialogue. The surveillance camera's own logic — fixed, patient, recording — is turned first into irony, then into poetry.

Gaps in This Archive

What This Archive Does Not Show

Every archive has limits. Part of being transparent about this project's methodology is naming what it cannot yet document, what it has chosen not to include, and what remains outside the frame of its current scope.

  • Five films in the archive — Wajib, May in the Summer, The Teacher, All That's Left of You, and Pomegranates and Myrrh — do not yet have completed close readings. Their inclusion as placeholders names the archive's incompleteness rather than concealing it.
  • The archive focuses on Palestinian women filmmakers, which means male directors whose work engages checkpoints — Elia Suleiman, Michel Khleifi, Hani Abu-Assad — appear only in supporting references rather than as primary subjects. That boundary is a methodological choice, not a claim that their work is less significant.
  • The Container checkpoint appears in the geographic archive without film cross-references beyond archive footage with dates TBD. Its inclusion marks a site of control that has not yet received the cinematic attention Qalandiya and Rachel have — which is itself analytically significant.
  • Documentary and social media documentation — including Bisan Owda's real-time witnessing — is referenced in the theoretical framework but not yet integrated as a formal archive category. The distinction between narrative film, documentary, and social media raises methodological questions the current archive does not fully resolve.
  • The bilingual Arabic/English translation currently covers the Surveillance Space page. The rest of the site remains English-only, which contradicts the project's commitment to refusing a single language of legibility.

The counter-surveillance filmmaker does not photograph occupation. She photographs life under it. And this archive does not map everything about Palestinian cinema or Palestinian space. It maps how Palestinian women filmmakers have insisted, across twenty-five years and across the specific geography of checkpoint sites, that Palestinian life exceeds the frame occupation tries to put around it.

What is not mapped here — the films not yet analyzed, the checkpoints not yet documented, the languages not yet supported — is not absence. It is the ongoing work of an archive that understands its own incompleteness as part of its argument. The project continues.
